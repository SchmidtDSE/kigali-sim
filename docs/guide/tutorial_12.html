Motivation

Beyond refrigerant choice, equipment energy efficiency plays a crucial role in the overall environmental impact of cooling systems. Energy-efficient equipment consumes less electricity, reducing both operational costs and indirect emissions from power generation. Modern high-efficiency refrigeration units can consume 30% less energy than older models while using the same refrigerant.

In this tutorial, we'll model an energy efficiency policy that gradually replaces high-energy equipment with low-energy alternatives. You'll see how this transition can significantly reduce overall energy consumption (measured in kWh) while maintaining the same refrigerant type and cooling capacity.

Setting Up the Business-as-Usual Scenario

Let's create our baseline scenario with both high-energy and low-energy models of the same HFC-134a refrigeration equipment. We'll start with high-energy models dominating the market and minimal low-energy model adoption.

Step 1: Create the Domestic Refrigeration application

  • Click Add Application
  • Name it "Domestic Refrigeration"
  • Click Save

Step 2: Add HFC-134a High Energy equipment model

  • In your Domestic Refrigeration application, click Add Substance
  • Name it "HFC-134a High Energy"
  • On the General tab:
    • Enable "Domestic Manufacture"
    • Set GWP to 1430 tCO2e/kg
    • Set Energy consumption to 500 kwh/unit
  • On the Equipment tab:
    • Set Initial charge to 0.15 kg/unit for Domestic Manufacture
    • Set Retirement rate to 5% each year
    • Set Recharge to 10% with 0.15 kg/unit each year
  • On the Set tab:
    • Set Prior equipment to 1,000,000 units in year 2025
    • Set Domestic Manufacture to 20 mt in year 2025
  • Click Save

Step 3: Add HFC-134a Low Energy equipment model

  • Still in your Domestic Refrigeration application, click Add Substance
  • Name it "HFC-134a Low Energy"
  • On the General tab:
    • Enable "Domestic Manufacture"
    • Set GWP to 1430 tCO2e/kg
    • Set Energy consumption to 350 kwh/unit
  • On the Equipment tab:
    • Set Initial charge to 0.15 kg/unit for Domestic Manufacture
    • Set Retirement rate to 5% each year
    • Set Recharge to 10% with 0.15 kg/unit each year
  • On the Set tab:
    • Set Prior equipment to 50,000 units in year 2025
    • Set Domestic Manufacture to 1 mt in year 2025
  • Click Save

Step 4: Create baseline simulation

  • Click Add Simulation
  • Name it "BAU"
  • Set duration from years 2025 to 2035
  • Leave all policies unchecked (this is our business-as-usual baseline)
  • Click Save

You should now see your baseline simulation running, showing the high-energy model as the dominant equipment with much higher consumption volumes than the low-energy alternative.

Adding the Energy Efficiency Policy

Now let's create a policy that gradually replaces high-energy equipment consumption with low-energy models. This will demonstrate how an equipment efficiency policy can reduce overall energy consumption without changing refrigerant type.

Step 1: Create the efficiency policy

  • Click Add Policy
  • Name it "Energy Efficiency"
  • Select Domestic Refrigeration as the application
  • Select HFC-134a High Energy as the substance

Step 2: Configure the replacement mechanism

  • Go to the Replace tab within your HFC-134a High Energy policy configuration
  • Click Add Replace
  • Set Replace 10% of sales with HFC-134a Low Energy
  • Set timing to each year during years 2028 to onwards
  • Click Save to finish the policy

This policy will progressively reduce high-energy equipment consumption by 10% each year starting in 2028, with that demand being met by low-energy equipment instead. Over time, this creates a significant shift toward more efficient equipment while maintaining the same refrigerant (HFC-134a) and overall cooling capacity.

Creating the Simulation

Now let's create a simulation to compare the policy scenario with our business-as-usual baseline.

  • Click Add Simulation
  • Name it "Efficiency Policy"
  • Check the Energy Efficiency policy checkbox
  • Set duration from years 2025 to 2035
  • Click Save

You should now see both your BAU and Efficiency Policy scenarios displayed side by side in the results panel.

Results

Let's examine how the efficiency policy affects both equipment adoption and energy consumption:

Equipment Model Consumption Changes

  • Select the Substances radio button to view individual equipment model consumption
  • Select the Consumption radio button to see total consumption volumes
  • Compare the BAU and Efficiency Policy simulations

You'll observe that starting in 2028, high-energy equipment consumption begins declining while low-energy equipment consumption increases correspondingly. The total refrigerant volume (when combined) remains similar between scenarios, but the equipment mix shifts dramatically toward the more efficient alternative.

Energy Consumption Comparison

  • Select the Equipment radio button and examine the energy consumption charts
  • Compare the BAU and Efficiency Policy simulations
  • Notice how overall energy consumption decreases significantly under the policy scenario

The energy benefits become apparent immediately: even though total equipment population and refrigerant consumption stay roughly constant, the overall kWh consumption drops substantially. This is because we're replacing equipment that consumes 500 kWh/year with units that consume only 350 kWh/year - a 30% efficiency improvement.

Understanding the Energy Efficiency Impact

The significant energy reduction demonstrates the importance of equipment efficiency policies. Each unit of high-energy equipment replaced with a low-energy alternative saves 150 kWh per year (500 - 350 = 150). This shows how equipment choice can substantially reduce energy consumption and associated indirect emissions while maintaining the same cooling services.

Climate and Economic Benefits

While the refrigerant consumption and direct emissions (tCO2e) remain similar between scenarios, the energy savings translate to reduced indirect emissions from electricity generation and lower operational costs for consumers. This demonstrates how energy efficiency complements refrigerant choice in comprehensive environmental impact reduction.

Conclusion

You've successfully modeled an equipment energy efficiency policy! This tutorial demonstrated:

  • Equipment differentiation: How the same refrigerant can have different environmental impacts based on equipment efficiency
  • Energy-focused policies: Using "replace X% of sales with substance" to model efficiency transitions
  • Indirect impact reduction: How energy efficiency reduces environmental impact beyond direct refrigerant emissions
  • Market transformation: Modeling progressive adoption of efficient technologies
  • Comprehensive assessment: Evaluating both direct and indirect environmental benefits

The energy efficiency policy shows how focusing on equipment characteristics alongside refrigerant choice can maximize overall environmental benefits. This approach is particularly relevant for comprehensive climate policies that address both direct emissions from refrigerants and indirect emissions from energy consumption.
